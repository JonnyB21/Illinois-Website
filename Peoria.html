Peoria, Illinois

  • Population: Approximately 111,000 (2023 estimate). Peoria is the seventh-largest city in Illinois and the largest city on the Illinois River. [1]
  • Founded: Established in 1691 by French explorers as Fort Crevecoeur, Peoria is the oldest European settlement in Illinois. It was incorporated as a town in 1835 and as a city in 1845. [1]
  • Region: Located in Central Illinois, Peoria sits along the Illinois River and serves as the county seat of Peoria County. The city is part of the Peoria Metropolitan Statistical Area, which includes several surrounding communities. [1]
  • Classification: Peoria is classified as an urban city and is a regional center for industry, healthcare, and education. It is known for its diverse neighborhoods, vibrant downtown, and strong community spirit. [1]
  • Average Household Income: Around $56,000 (U.S. Census Bureau). The city has a mix of income levels, with both affluent suburbs and working-class neighborhoods. [2]

Peoria is renowned for its picturesque riverfront, historic districts, and cultural attractions such as the Peoria Riverfront Museum and the Peoria Civic Center. The city has a rich industrial heritage, most notably as the longtime headquarters of Caterpillar Inc., a Fortune 100 company. Peoria is also home to Bradley University, a nationally recognized private university, and several major hospitals, making it a hub for education and healthcare in the region.

The city’s location along the Illinois River has made it a key transportation and trading center since its founding. Today, Peoria offers a blend of urban amenities and natural beauty, with numerous parks, trails, and outdoor recreation opportunities. Its diverse economy, strong sense of community, and central location have earned Peoria the reputation of being a “typical American city”—a place where new products and ideas are often tested to see if they will succeed nationwide.
